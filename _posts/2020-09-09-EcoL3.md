Notes and exercises on market equilibrium and structures.

### Equilibrium

a point at which there is no tendency for the market price to change as long as exogenous variables remain unchanged (market clearing price). The supply and demand curves must intercept.

- Exercise C0

  Demand is given by $q^D(p) = 1200-p$, supply is $q^S(p) = 300+2p$. The market equilibrium point is?

  Solution:

  $$q^D(p) = q^S(p)$$
  yields: 
  $$\begin{aligned} q^* &= 900 \\  p^* &= 300 \end{aligned}$$

  at 5€: The demand is **excess demand**.  At this price, the supply is less than the demand.

  at 10€: The supply is **excess supply**. Now the demand is less than the supply.

### Perfectly Competitive Markets

These are the features of it:

- The **market demand curve** reflects the **utility** that all consumers derive from the consumption of a good.

- The **market supply curve** includes all **costs** that producers of an industry incur for the production of a good.

- Price = Marginal Cost

- Firms operate at maximum efficiency

- Due to the **atomistic market structure**, no consumer or producer is large enough to exert influence on the market price (no market power!). Market price can adjust freely to balance demand and supply.

  > Atomistic market structure: A market structure where firms are so numerous that the market represents perfect competition.

### Monopoly

These are few points about it:

1. Horizontal or vertical mergers or takeovers
2. Acquisition of control over key input (electrical networks/gas pipelines...)
3. Tactics, such as **predatory pricing**
4. Lower production cost than competitors. e.g., economies of scale in production (*natural monopoly*)
5. High barriers to entry
6. Patents on products
7. Government granted or legally created monopolies

Max Profit:

$Maximizing: \ p\cdot q(p) - C(q(p))$

- Exercise C5: Monopolist

  Shift in demand and elasticity--Consider a D and a D' curve. D' has a higher demand elasticity than D yields:
  $$\frac{\Delta q_{D^\prime}}{\Delta p_{D^\prime}} > \frac{\Delta q_D}{\Delta p_D}$$
  which means q reacts more to p.

- Exercise D1:

  A monopolist faces production cost of $C(q) = 0.25q^2$. The electricity producer acts as a monopolist, i.e. there are no competitors in the market. The monopolist faces demand, given by the inverse demand function $p(q) = 100-2q$. Profit maximizing quantity and price ?

  - solution:

    $$\begin{aligned} \pi(q) &= (100-2q)q - 0.25^2 \\ &= 100q-2.25q^2\end{aligned}$$
    
    $\pi'(q) = 100-4.5q = 0$ find the maximum point of $\pi(q)$ by taking derivative of it with respect to q.

    *Taking derivative with respect to q is an alternative way.*

    yielding:
    
    $q^*= 22.22, p^* = 55.6, \pi = 1111$

- Exercise D2: Also Monopolist

  A electricity producer has a cost of  $C(q) = 1.5q^2 + 15q$. And it is a monopolist. The demand is (given by the inverse demand function): $p(q) = 90-4.5q$. What are the profit maximizing point ?

  - solution:

    Revenue $R(q) = 90q - 4.5q^2$

    Profit $\pi(q) = (90-4.5q)q-(1.5q^2 + 15q)$

    Marginal revenue $R'(q) = 90-9q$

    Marginal cost $C'(q) = 3q+15$

    $C'(q) = R'(q) \rightarrow q^* = 6.25, p^* = 61.875$

    So $\pi = 234.375$

    In this case of monopolist, the MC is not equal to demand. Because this is not a perfectly competitive market. In a perfectly competitive market, we can solve that $q^*= 10, p^* = 45, \pi^* = 150$

### Oligopoly

1. Few producers, many consumers.
2. Strategic interaction of producers--Each producer’s profits are affected by actions of other producers and vice versa.
3. Games with simultaneous and sequential decision making.
4. Simultaneous move games: **Cournot** and **Bertrand**.
5. Simplification: duopoly – only 2 producers.
