## Arguments for deregulation-liberalization 
### Theoretical background
> Welfare economics suggests that policy should usually aim to maximize the sum of consumer and producer welfare , while the public interest theory of regulation implies that regulatory decisions are generally taken to further such an objective.--Green,2006
Definitions:
- Social Welfare: Sum of welfare of individuals (e.g. producers and consumers). Total Welfare = Consumer Surplus + Producer Surplus.
- Pareto Efficiency: Allocation in which no person can be made better o without making someone else worse off. 
- Allocative Efficiency: Optimal distribution of goods and services, reflecting consumer preferences and cost of production(marginal utility(p) = marginal cost)
- Productive efficnency: : Maximum number of goods and services produced with given amount of inputs.
- Neo-classic economics states that under perfect competition:
    - The quantities offered on a market are optimal
    - Market prices = marginal cost of production
    - Allocation of resources is the best

Under perfect competition, Surplus producer and surplus consumer are Maximised = **Social welfare is maximised**, allocative and productive efficiencies are maximised. The competition drives the market prices down and increases the quantity of goods and services offered, and quality is improved.

_In PPC, the Producer and the consumer’s surpluses are maximized at the equilibrium = welfare maximization, or social optimum._

### Welfare in Oligopoly
> The Cournot Oligopoly are:
> - Quantities: competition in quantities between producers leads to a greater output level than under monopoly, but lower than under perfect competition.
> - Prices: By the same token, prices are lower than under monopoly but higher than under perfect competition.
> - Welfare: Welfare is higher than in monopoly because output is higher.
> - Overall: With more priducers in oligopoly we move towards perfect competition - output is increased and so is allocative efficnency.

### Exercise G1
Producers and isolated markets.

An energy market is regulated by authorities by setting price caps equal to marginal production costs. No new producer entry to the market is allowed. It is assumed that the regulator has full information about costs and demand.

Producers:

$$\begin{aligned}
    C_1(q) &= 20 + 2q + 1.5q^2 \\
    C_2(q) &= 1.5q
    \end{aligned}$$

Contraint: $q_1 \leq 20$

Demand: $D(p) = 40$

1. Find the equilibrium price and quantity for the market? And the profit?

Demand is fixed at 40 and is inelastic(vertical demand curce).➡ Market is regulated to avoid exercise of market power. ➡ Price cap is identical for both producers, but corresponds to the MC.

Find MCs:

$$\begin{aligned}
    {C_2}'(q) &= 1.5 \\
    {C_1}'(q) &= 2 +3q
    \end{aligned}$$

Producer 2 produces at full capacity(20) because its MC is always cheaper.
Producer 1 produces the rest of the demand, 20.

Therefore, the solution for this market:

$$\begin{aligned}
    q_1 &= 20 \\
    p &= MC = 2 +3q_1 = 62
    \end{aligned}$$

The profits:

$$\begin{aligned}
    P_1 &= 62\times 20 - 20 - 2\times 20 - 1.5\times 20^2=580 \\
    P_2 &= 62\times 20 -1.5\times 20 = 1210
    \end{aligned}$$

2. How is this solution for the market compared to a fully competitive market solution assuming that this could be established with just two producers.

Comparing this regulated solution to a perfect competition solution, they are the same as the regulator sets the maximum allowed price at exactly the mc for the supplier that supplies the last unit to the market. **This type of regulation is only possible if the regulator has good information about the cost characteristics for the producers**(their marginal cost curves).

### Exercise G2
Market solution in another region.

Another region (country) has a market for the same energy good also with two producers. Prices here are also regulated so that MC (of market supply) = price.

The North and South producers costs and the market demand:

$$\begin{aligned}
    C_N(q) &= 1 + 0.5q + 3q^2 \\
    C_S(q) &= 3q + 2.25q^2
    D(p) &= 200-2p
    \end{aligned}$$

1. Find the equilibrium price and quantity for the market.

Find the MC first:

$$\begin{aligned}
    {C_N}'(q) &= 0.5 +6q\\
    {C_S}'(q) &= 3 +4.5q
    \end{aligned}$$

These two MCs are linear. They should be added horizontally to find the supply curve.

> Contrary to Ex G1 the marginal cost curves of the two producers are now overlapping and we have a downward sloping demand curve. This means that **both producers will contribute to the market supply curve** for all _prices_ above 3. It also means that their marginal cost have to be equal at the market equilibrium.

The added marginal cost curve (market supply curve) will start with North supply curve and at price p=3 will continue as the sum of North and South supply.

The simple way of finding the market supply curve is to take 2 prices and calculate the supply from each producer at this price. This is done by inserting the price (e. 10) in the MC functions for north and south, finding the q's and adding. The market supply is then the sum of the two supplies for that specific price level.

![G2](/assets/pics/eco6_G2.png)

Alternative way of finding supply curve? Find the quantities first and add them together:

$$\begin{aligned}
    q_N &= \frac{p-0.5}{6} \\
    q_S &= \frac{p-3}{4.5} \\
    q &= q_N + q_S = \frac{7}{18}p - \frac{3}{4} \\
    & \text{and reverse q back to price....} \\
    p &= \frac{18}{7}q + 1.929 \ \text{this is the combined surve}
    \end{aligned}$$

This combined curve(the green one) only is starting from the price where both suppliers contribute. At p = 3, the supply curve change slope from 6 to 18/7.

When p = 3, ${C_N}'(q) = 0.5 +6q = 3$ ➡ q_N = 0.42. This is the starting of the aggregate supply curve.

Solving:

$$\begin{aligned}
    1.93 + \frac{18}{7}q &= \frac{200-q}{2} \\
    \textit{yields:} \ 
    q &= 31.92 \\
    p &= 84.04 = MC_N = MC_S
    \end{aligned}$$

Profit:

$$\begin{aligned}
    MC_N &= 84.04 = 0.5 + 6q_N \rightarrow q_N = 13.92 \\
    MC_S &= 84.04 = 3 + 4.5q_S \rightarrow q_S = 18.01 \\
    & P_N = 84.04\times 13.92 - 1 - 0.5 \times 13.92 - 3\times 13.92^2 = 580.58 \\
    & P_S = 84.04\times 18.01 - 3\times 18.01 - 2.25\times 18.01^2 = 729.72
    \end{aligned}$$

So, Producer S has an advantage in lower growth in production costs with increasing output as compared to producer N and this is reflected both in production and profits.
